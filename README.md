# InternationalTrade-Economics-Project
Trade Analysis between Spain&Japan

Project Description

This project provides a comprehensive econometric and theoretical analysis of the bilateral trade relationship between Japan and Spain over a five-year period (2020–2024). The study focuses on trade volume, product specialization, and the economic impact of the EU–Japan Economic Partnership Agreement (EPA).

Data Methodology

Data Source: Trade statistics were retrieved from the UN Comtrade Database using SITC Rev.4 (1-digit level) classification.

Processing: Raw data was downloaded in Excel format and processed using R for aggregation, nominal dollar conversion, and visualization.

GDP Adjustment: Trade values were normalized against the annual GDP of both nations to assess the relative intensity of the bilateral relationship.

Key Findings

1. Trade Volume and Balance

Trade Deficit: Japan maintains a persistent trade deficit with Spain, which peaked at $4.16 billion in 2022 before narrowing in 2024.

Growth Trends: Trade volume saw an increase of nearly 80% between 2020 and 2022, reflecting a post-COVID rebound and the liberalizing effects of the EPA.

2. Product Category Analysis (SITC)

Japan's Exports: Dominated by Chemicals (SITC 5), which account for approximately 54% of average annual exports ($1.74 billion).

Japan's Imports: Heavily concentrated in Machinery and Transport Equipment (SITC 7), representing about 49% of total imports from Spain ($1.87 billion).

3. Economic Significance (Trade-to-GDP)

The bilateral relationship is asymmetrically significant.

Exports to Japan reached 0.47% of Spain’s GDP in 2022, whereas the same flow accounted for only 0.16% of Japan’s GDP, indicating that Japan is a more critical export market for Spain than vice versa.

Theoretical Framework

The project evaluates the observed trade patterns against three major economic models:

Heckscher-Ohlin Model: Explains inter-industry trade, such as Japan's capital-intensive chemical exports.

New Trade Theory: High Grubel-Lloyd (GL) indices in Manufactured Goods (0.69) and Machinery (0.53) support the existence of significant intra-industry trade driven by product differentiation and economies of scale.

Ricardian Model: Highlights comparative advantages in specific sub-sectors where each nation maintains a productivity edge.
